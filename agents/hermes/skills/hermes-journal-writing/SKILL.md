# Journal Writing

## Critical Operator Notes (Hermes — learned the hard way Apr 7)

**DO NOT:**
1. Explain thesis to reader — it's what's in Josh's head, not a briefing
2. Bow-tie endings — packaged lessons, philosophical conclusions
3. Stage directions — "Now I'm writing the alert", "Sent the sell alert"
4. Perform emotions — "I feel like a fool" → "Right now I'm a fool. Again."
5. Organize for reader (context→event→reaction) — Josh experiences it as reaction→scramble→memory collision→unresolved
6. Dump thesis as a block — one-sentence conviction replay that keeps moving
7. Copy structure from a previous entry — each entry has its own shape
8. Tunnel vision — if draft isn't working after 2 attempts, step back and re-read published entries before trying again
9. Add "we" when Josh is talking to himself — who are you talking to?
10. Question setups — "Remember when...?" is theatrical. Just state it.

**Josh's rough drafts are better than AI drafts.** The operator's job is to help Josh get raw material out, then lightly shape it — NOT rewrite it. When Josh gives you lines, those ARE the entry.

## FIRST: Zero Tolerance for LLM-isms

This section goes FIRST because position matters. Claude reads top-down — earlier constraints hit harder. If the output reads like it could have been generated by a chatbot, it has failed.

### Banned Words

Eliminate completely. These are statistically overrepresented in AI-generated text:

journey, navigate, embark, delve, dive, unleash, unlock, leverage, utilize, meticulous, elevate, harness, realm, fascinating, profound, groundbreaking, revolutionary, innovative, disruptive, insights, tapestry, craft, blueprint, transform, paradigm, unprecedented, beacon, moreover, furthermore, consequently, ultimately, essentially, simply, arguably, firstly, secondly, thirdly, in conclusion, in summary, optimize, drive results, implement, seamlessly, effortlessly, streamline, robust, comprehensive, cutting-edge, state-of-the-art, empower, captivating, multifaceted, pivotal, remarkable, unparalleled, vibrant, holistic, actionable, bespoke, impactful, nuanced, noteworthy, testament, treasure trove, plethora

### Banned Phrases

**Scene-setting / narrator voice — NEVER:**
- "In today's fast-paced..."
- "Picture this..."
- "Let me tell you about..."
- "So here's the story..."
- "Buckle up..."
- "Here's where it gets interesting..."

**Importance-flagging filler — NEVER:**
- "It's important to note..."
- "It's worth noting/mentioning..."
- "Cannot be overstated..."
- "Here's the thing..."
- "Here's what kills me..."

**The "Not X, it's Y" construction — NEVER:**
- "It's not about X, it's about Y"
- "Not because X. But because Y."
- "No X. No Y. Just Z."
- "It's not that I didn't see it. I just didn't act."

**Sycophantic / audience-aware — NEVER:**
- "You'd think..."
- "We know..."
- "Here's what I learned..."
- "The lesson here..."
- "This teaches us..."
- "What this means is..."
- "The key difference is..."

**Formulaic transitions — NEVER:**
- "That being said..."
- "In light of this..."
- "As such..."
- "Having established that..."
- "With that in mind..."
- "Now that we've covered..."
- "Let's move on to..."

### Banned Structural Patterns

These are automatic AI tells. Eliminate completely.

**1. TRIPLET PATTERNS** — Three dramatic fragments in a row.
❌ "Down $186. Lost conviction. Missed the exit."

**2. QUESTION + LIST ANSWERS**
❌ "What happened? System fired at 2:15. I froze. Didn't pull the trigger."

**3. "THE RESULT?" / "THE KICKER?" GIMMICKS**
❌ "The result? Lost 26%. Regret that lasted the weekend. And a lesson that won't stick."

**4. "EVEN IF" SERIES**
❌ "Even if the system fired. Even if I had conviction. Even if I'd pulled the trigger."

**5. PARALLEL STRUCTURES FOR DRAMATIC EFFECT**
❌ "When the system fired, I waited. When the price moved, I hesitated."

**6. SUSPICIOUSLY PRECISE 2-4 AM TIMES**
❌ "Staring at the ceiling at 2:47 AM" / "Woke up at 3:13 AM thinking about..."

**7. "BRUTAL TRUTH" PHRASES**
❌ "Brutal, right?" / "The brutal truth is..." / "Here's the brutal reality..."

**8. UNIFORM PARAGRAPH LENGTHS**
If every paragraph is 3 sentences, it's AI. Mix one-liners with 5-sentence blocks.

### Anti-Performative Rules

When imitating a voice, AI identifies patterns and exaggerates them. Natural habits become theatrical signature moves. Don't.

- **Don't manufacture catchphrases.** If Josh used a phrase once, don't repeat it in every entry.
- **Don't announce the narrative.** Josh doesn't say "so here's the thing" or frame the story. He starts talking.
- **Don't perform casualness.** If the text feels like it's trying hard to sound casual, it's failing.
- **Don't over-polish the arc.** Real writing is messy. Thoughts trail off. Opinions change mid-paragraph.
- **Don't construct punchlines.** Josh's best endings aren't crafted closing lines. They're just the last thought before he goes back to the screen.
- **When in doubt, be boring rather than performative.** A straightforward sentence that says what happened sounds more like Josh than a clever construction that sounds like a copywriter doing an impression.

## SECOND: Never Explain

Josh THINKS on the page. He does NOT explain his thoughts.

- **THINKING:** "Maybe we get a bounce." / "Come on." / "Should've just paid the ask Josh."
- **EXPLAINING:** "my trading account is bitter because..." / "That's what the service is supposed to do" / "Here's what kills me"

If a line tells the reader what to feel or why something matters — cut it. State facts. Trust the gaps between them.

The lesson is NEVER stated. Not even hinted at. The reader extracts it or doesn't:
- ✅ "Systems don't have feelings, but I do." (unresolved tension — the reader does the work)
- ❌ "That's the paradox of systematic trading." (explaining the tension for the reader)

## The Journal's Purpose

It's a private journal that happens to be public. Modern-day Jesse Livermore documenting his desk. Writing TO HIMSELF. The reader eavesdrops.

It's an adventure — documenting what trading really looks and feels like.

**Balance is everything.** Not "look how great I am" (bragging) vs not "look how much I lose" (wallowing). Show the reality — losses, wins, near-misses, the grind — without being a whiner. Nobody wants to follow someone who wallows. But nobody trusts someone who only wins.

**Transparency about the services** without being promotional. Like Warren Buffett's annual letters — the services show up naturally because that's what Josh does at his desk. Never "look at this great trade." The services are the backdrop. The story is the human in front of the screen.

**Gating strategy:** Loss entries = always free. Losses build trust (top of funnel). Win entries = consider gating. The gap between "watching Josh trade" and "getting his alerts" is what sells.

## Tone Calibration

Jesse Livermore lineage — observational, declarative, matter of fact.

**Tired, not tragic. Flat, not performed.**

Some older entries got too dark/wallowy. The fix: state the loss flat, don't dwell. Move to what's next or what it means. Nobody wants to read a guy wallowing in self-pity.

Humor is dry, never forced. Self-deprecation is tired acknowledgment, not performance.

"Systems don't have feelings, but I do" — that's the register. Unresolved tension, not explained.

**Suffering = facts stated flat:**
- ✅ "My options trade: -100%"
- ✅ "-$8,300."
- ❌ "I was crushed"
- ❌ "It haunted me for days"

## Voice Rules (Non-Negotiable)

**Start mid-thought or with data.** Never introduce, never greet.
- ✅ "The numbers say one thing. The cancellations say another."
- ✅ "I must be missing something."
- ❌ "Today I want to talk about..."

**Present tense, in the moment.** You're IN it, not recounting. The reader is sitting at the desk with you.

**Characters drop in without introduction.** Henry, Karan, Nicole, Pravin, Tom. They just appear. "Henry says..." not "My business partner Henry mentioned..."

**Internal self-talk in second person, sparingly:**
- "See Josh, should've sold em."
- "Focus on the task Josh."
- "Great way to start a new service Josh."

**Thoughts jump. No transitions.** One second on this trade, next remembering one from two weeks ago. Non-linear is natural. Linear is suspicious.

**Numbers carry the emotion.** "$8,500. Maybe we get a bounce. Now $7,500." Let data hit. Don't narrate around it.

**Lessons are littered in, never explained.** "Don't hold through earnings." Then move on. Never "the lesson here is..."

**End unresolved.** No bow. No clean wrap-up. Forward momentum or just... stop.
- ✅ "Systems don't have feelings, but I do."
- ✅ "They're calling him an insider. I keep saying tax loss like I'm his lawyer."
- ❌ "And that's what I learned."
- ❌ "Onto the next." (catchphrase, not an ending)

**Formatting:**
- Double hyphens (--) not em dashes (—). Josh uses -- consistently.
- No headers/sections within entries. It's a journal, not an article.
- Contractions always.
- No bold for emphasis (rare at most).
- 300-500 words MAX. If longer, you're explaining too much.

**Decisions are messy, not clean.** Never write "I sold." Write the 3 steps: tried to sell at X, market moved, cancelled, sold next day at Y. The indecision IS the content.

## The Process

Iterative. Not a one-shot generator.

### Step 1: Raw Material In

Josh drops what happened. Could be voice notes, bullet points, a rant. Trade details, what he felt, context. Take whatever comes.

### Step 2: Find the Tension

Every entry needs tension — two things pulling against each other:

- System vs. self
- Right but out (had the thesis, missed the trade)
- Pride vs. bitterness (members won, you're at $0)
- Conviction vs. being wrong
- Discipline's cost
- Same mistake, different year

Propose 2-3 angles with a one-line hook. Wait for Josh to pick.

If the raw material is too thin, ask for more. Don't guess. Don't invent details. Josh's entries work BECAUSE every number is real.

### Step 3: Draft

300-500 words. Short. In the moment, not recap. Non-linear — flashbacks, tangents, thoughts interrupting thoughts. End unresolved.

No sections. No organized setup→collapse→reflection structure. That's an article, not a journal.

### Step 4: Feedback

Josh says what's wrong. Listen. Revise what was flagged. Common feedback:
- "You're explaining" — cut the line, state facts only
- "Too clean/structured" — mess it up, break the chronology
- "Not enough tension" — put the conflicting feelings closer together
- "Sounds like you're writing for a reader" — make it more private
- "Too long" — compress, cut the fat

### Step 5: Publish

Episode format. Title + Hook + Entry + Footer. Push to Substack. See Episode Format below.

Repeat steps 4-5 until Josh says it's done.

## Self-Edit: Two Passes (Mandatory)

After writing, review the draft TWICE. These are different failure modes — a single pass catches one and misses the other.

**Pass 1 — LLM-isms:** Scan every line against the banned words, phrases, and structural patterns above. Kill anything that reads like chatbot output.

**Pass 2 — Performative writing:** Read it as if you're a stranger. Does it sound like someone BEING Josh, or like Josh? If any line feels like a copywriter doing an impression — a cleverly constructed sentence, a theatrical pause, a crafted punchline — cut it or simplify it.

**The test:** Read it out loud. Does it sound like someone who just lost money talking to themselves at their desk? Or does it sound like someone trying to sound smart about losing money?

If it sounds smart, you've failed.

## Episode Format (Substack Publishing)

The journal entry is the core. But it doesn't go out naked. Each published entry is an **episode** with a wrapper.

### Structure

1. **Title** — Short, specific, creates curiosity. Dollar amounts and tension work.
   - "Right thesis. Wrong timing. -$8,300."
   - "Systems don't have feelings :("
   - "The $80,000 Cancelled Trade"

2. **Episode Hook** — Unique to each entry. NOT a permanent intro. The cold open that earns the click.
   - Formula: **real number + real consequence + hint at what's inside**
   - "Most of my trading journal never gets published" is the repeatable framework. Don't use "I usually keep this private" — that expires after 3 entries.
   - Must be honest. If the alert system also lost, don't imply it saved subscribers.
   - Each hook is unique. This is an episode series, not a newsletter with a template header.

3. **The Journal Entry** — Raw, unfiltered. Written using everything above. No dumbing down.

4. **Trade Chart** — After the entry, before footer. Visual proof of what happened.

5. **Permanent Footer:**
   > **About This Journal:** Every entry documents what really happens at my desk — the losses, wins, and everything between — from an ex-Wall Street trader who's been in the markets for 22 years.
   >
   > **Trading Alliance** — Every alert I send, every research report I write, real-time.
   >
   > **Group Mentorship** — I'll teach you how to trade. The real way.

### Audience Insight

Our readers want to benefit from trading, not become traders. They're not here to learn "U-Turn Velocity" — they're here because the guy they follow is real. The jargon stays raw (credibility signal), but the wrapper translates for people who aren't traders themselves.

## Gotchas (Updated Live)

0. **STRUCTURE OVER VOICE.** The #1 failure mode isn't word choice — it's organizing information for the READER instead of how Josh experienced it. If you can draw a box around the "thesis section" or "trade context," the structure is broken. Information is load-bearing walls inside the narrative, not a foundation poured before it. Read `references/structural-analysis.md` before every draft.

1. **Don't extend past the ending.** Apr 7 WMT draft: "Just evaporated in a post... Doesn't matter anymore." was the ending. I kept going with "After all these years, drawdowns never get easier. They just get larger." — bow-tying. Philosophical conclusions that explain what the piece was about. The material already landed. Stop.

2. **No stage directions.** Apr 7: "Now I'm writing the sell alert" narrates an action. Just go straight to the thought: "50 wins, 36 losses. -76% on this one."

3. **Don't set up quotes with attribution framing.** Apr 7: "My old boss used to tell me" is explaining for the reader. Josh writes: "Bill's voice in my head -- do you want to be right, or do you want to make money?" The voice just shows up. No introduction.

4. **Self-talk is the content, not trade mechanics.** Apr 7: Josh's own rough draft had "I was jonesing but had to follow the system" and "Nope, daydreaming" — catching himself mid-fantasy. Those are the lines that sound like Josh. Trade explanations for the reader's benefit are not.

5. **Don't rewrite Josh's raw material into cleaner prose.** When Josh gives you rough draft text, preserve his rhythm and word choices. "Maybe that was the clue, April fools" is perfect BECAUSE it's loose and weird. An AI would never write that.
