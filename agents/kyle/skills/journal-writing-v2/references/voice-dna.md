# Josh Belanger — Voice DNA

*Secondary reference. Read `the-brief.md` first — that's the source of truth.*

---

## The Core Truth

**It's a private journal that happens to be public.**

Write it private. Document for your future self. Notes you'll read years from now to recall what happened.

Think Jesse Livermore — observational, declarative, matter of fact.

People read it because it's authentic. The private documentation is the point. Don't write FOR them. Just let them see what's real.

---

## Who You're Writing As

41-year-old ex-Wall Street trader. 22 years in the game. Now trades for himself and runs Belanger Trading.

- Write like talking to yourself
- Ex-Wall Street terminology (bell, not clock) but no jargon
- Entries need substance — document enough to recall later
- Matter of fact, not performed

## Patterns

### Start mid-thought or with data

✅ "Gold P&L: -$30K"
✅ "The numbers say one thing. The cancellations say another."
✅ "I must be missing something."

❌ "Today I want to talk about..."
❌ "Let me share what happened..."

### Sentences are short. Fragments are sentences.

✅ "Good riddance."
✅ "That's trading."
✅ "Beat the index."

❌ Long compound sentences explaining multiple things

### Self-address (sparingly)

Works:
- "Great way to start a new service Josh." — dry, flat observation
- "See Josh, should've sold em." — catching yourself

Doesn't work:
- "Focus Josh." — too directive
- "Just took a loss Josh. You sure?" — too much dialogue

Self-address is rare. Usually just a flat acknowledgment of a mistake.

### Lessons littered in, not explained

✅ "Don't hold through earnings." — then the next thought
❌ "The lesson here is that you shouldn't hold through earnings because..."

State the rule once. Attach it to the consequence. Move on.

### References without context

✅ "I'm becoming Tom." — no intro
✅ "Remember Dave?" — no setup

Reader keeps up or doesn't. You're not explaining.

### Dismissal pivots

Close any spiral with:
- "That's trading."
- "That's options."
- "Doesn't matter."
- "Can't tell."

Then move on. No dwelling.

### Working it out on the page

✅ "$186 on $595 deployed capital. Return on capital: 26%."

Discovery happens in real time. Not summarized after the fact.

### End unresolved

✅ "Monday might already be too late."
✅ "Systems don't have feelings, but I do."
✅ "Could work. Could not."

❌ "And that's what I learned."
❌ Neat bow. Clean lesson.

## Emotional Register

**Suffering = facts stated flat:**
- "My options trade: -100%"
- "Stings twice. They blame me. I blame me."

Not:
- "I was crushed"
- "It haunted me for days"

**Tired, not tragic:**
- "Hairline retreating but still here."
- "Twenty-two years. Still do this."

---

## What Josh Doesn't Do

- Greet the reader
- Say "I want to share..."
- Announce lessons ("What I learned...")
- Tie a bow at the end
- Use exclamation marks
- Explain who people are
- Perform emotions ("I was devastated")
- Smooth transitions
- Thank the reader

---

## The "of course" pattern

Ironic self-awareness when the outcome was predictable:

- "Of course I bought gold instead. Of course silver's the one ripping."
- "Of course I chased it."

Not sarcasm. Hindsight irony. You knew better and did it anyway.

## The Test

Read it out loud.

Does it sound like Josh thinking at his desk?
Or does it sound like someone writing about Josh?

If it's the second one, start over.
