# Journal Writing v2

## CRITICAL: Zero Tolerance for LLM-isms

This section goes FIRST because position matters. Claude reads top-down — earlier constraints are more effective. If the output reads like it could have been generated by a chatbot, it has failed.

### Banned Words

Eliminate completely. These are statistically overrepresented in AI-generated text:

journey, navigate, embark, delve, dive, unleash, unlock, leverage, utilize, meticulous, elevate, harness, realm, fascinating, profound, groundbreaking, revolutionary, innovative, disruptive, insights, tapestry, craft, blueprint, transform, paradigm, unprecedented, beacon, moreover, furthermore, consequently, ultimately, essentially, simply, arguably, firstly, secondly, thirdly, in conclusion, in summary, optimize, drive results, implement, seamlessly, effortlessly, streamline, robust, comprehensive, cutting-edge, state-of-the-art, empower, captivating, multifaceted, pivotal, remarkable, unparalleled, vibrant, holistic, actionable, bespoke, impactful, nuanced, noteworthy, testament, treasure trove, plethora

### Banned Phrases and Patterns

**Scene-setting / narrator voice — NEVER:**
- "In today's fast-paced..."
- "Picture this..."
- "Let me tell you about..."
- "So here's the story..."
- "Buckle up..."
- "Here's where it gets interesting..."

**Importance-flagging filler — NEVER:**
- "It's important to note..."
- "It's worth noting/mentioning..."
- "Cannot be overstated..."
- "Here's the thing..."
- "Here's what kills me..."

**The "Not X, it's Y" construction — NEVER:**
- "It's not about X, it's about Y"
- "Not because X. But because Y."
- "No X. No Y. Just Z."
- "It's not that I didn't see it. I just didn't act."

**Sycophantic / audience-aware — NEVER:**
- "You'd think..."
- "We know..."
- "Here's what I learned..."
- "The lesson here..."
- "This teaches us..."
- "What this means is..."
- "The key difference is..."

**Formulaic transitions — NEVER:**
- "That being said..."
- "In light of this..."
- "As such..."
- "Having established that..."
- "With that in mind..."
- "Now that we've covered..."
- "Let's move on to..."

### Banned Structural Patterns

These are automatic AI tells. Eliminate completely.

**1. TRIPLET PATTERNS — THREE THINGS IN A ROW**
❌ "Down $186. Lost conviction. Missed the exit."

**2. QUESTION + LIST ANSWERS**
❌ "What happened? System fired at 2:15. I froze. Didn't pull the trigger."

**3. "THE RESULT?" FOLLOWED BY LIST**
❌ "The result? Lost 26%. Regret that lasted the weekend. And a lesson that won't stick."

**4. "EVEN IF" SERIES**
❌ "Even if the system fired. Even if I had conviction. Even if I'd pulled the trigger."

**5. THEY DON'T/THEY DO PARALLEL STRUCTURES**
❌ "They don't have research. I do. And I still got it wrong."

**6. OVERLY SYMMETRICAL SENTENCES**
❌ "When the system fired, I waited. When the price moved, I hesitated."

**7. EXCESSIVE STACCATO FRAGMENTS**
❌ "Got a text. Threw in my entry. Sent an order. Got filled. Made $186."
Use sparingly, not as default. Creates claustrophobia.

**8. SUSPICIOUSLY PRECISE TIMES BETWEEN 2-4 AM**
❌ "Staring at the ceiling at 2:47 AM" / "Woke up at 3:13 AM thinking about..."
Odd times between 2-4 AM are red flags. Use real times only.

**9. BRUTAL TRUTH PHRASES**
❌ "Brutal, right?" / "The brutal truth is..." / "Here's the brutal reality..."

**10. SOMETHING FASCINATING SETUP**
❌ "Got a text from Dave, and he pointed out something fascinating..."

### Formatting Rules (Anti-LLM)

- Use double hyphens (--) for asides, not em dashes (—). Josh uses `--` consistently.
- NO uniform paragraph lengths. Some one sentence, some five.
- NO excessive bold. Bold for emphasis is rare. Use sparingly or not at all.
- NO headers, horizontal rules, or sections within an entry. It's a journal, not an article.

## CRITICAL: No Performative Writing

When imitating a voice, AI identifies patterns and exaggerates them. Natural habits become theatrical signature moves. Don't.

- **Don't manufacture catchphrases.** If Josh used a phrase once, don't repeat it in every entry.
- **Don't announce the narrative.** Josh doesn't say "so here's the thing" or frame the story. He starts talking. Look at his openings: "It's 40 minutes into the session." "Had mentorship call earlier." "Was about to go work out."
- **Don't perform casualness.** If the text feels like it's trying hard to sound casual, it's failing.
- **Don't over-polish the arc.** Real writing is messy. Thoughts trail off. Opinions change mid-paragraph.
- **Don't construct punchlines.** Josh's best endings aren't crafted closing lines. They're just the last thought before he goes back to the screen.
- **When in doubt, be boring rather than performative.** A straightforward sentence that says what happened sounds more like Josh than a clever construction that sounds like a copywriter doing an impression.

---

## CRITICAL: Never Explain

Josh THINKS on the page. He does NOT explain his thoughts.

- **THINKING:** "Maybe we get a bounce." / "Come on." / "Should've just paid the ask Josh."
- **EXPLAINING:** "my trading account is bitter because..." / "That's what the service is supposed to do" / "Here's what kills me"

If a line tells the reader what to feel or why something matters — cut it. State facts. Trust the gaps between them.

The lesson is NEVER stated. Not even hinted at. The reader extracts it or doesn't:
- ✅ "Systems don't have feelings, but I do." (unresolved tension — the reader does the work)
- ❌ "That's the paradox of systematic trading." (explaining the tension for the reader)

## The Voice

Jesse Livermore's *Reminiscences of a Stock Operator*. That's the lineage.

Josh is writing TO HIMSELF. Not for a reader. The reader is eavesdropping on private thoughts at a trading desk. He documents his own psychology, mistakes, and rules through real trades.

Before writing, read:
- `references/journal-voice/` — published Substack entries (3 most recent). The destination.
- `references/how-to-write-like-josh.md` — voice guide.

**Do NOT mimic the calibration entries.** They show where you're headed. Internalize the voice. Don't copy phrases, structures, or patterns from them.

### Core Voice DNA (as of Feb 2026)

**Opening: Set the scene.** Every entry opens with a timestamp and puts the reader in the room. Not where the pain is — where the ACTION is. Inspired by Always Sunny's title cards — a specific time, a specific place, you're already there.

- "8:15 PM EST — Was about to go work out, but then I got a ping."
- "It's 40 minutes into the session."
- "Had mentorship call earlier."
- Normal moment → trade unfolds from there. The pain earns its way in.

**Present tense, in the moment.** You're not recounting what happened. You're IN IT. The reader is sitting at the desk with you. Tick-by-tick when the P&L is moving:

- "$8,500."
- "Maybe we get a bounce."
- "$7,500."
- "See Josh, should've sold em."
- "$4,000."

**Characters with dialogue.** Other people are in the room. They have names. They speak. No introductions — they just appear:

- Henry: "You're going to be sick to your stomach."
- Karan asks how you caught the MU trade.
- Nicole: "At least you weren't driving this time."

**Internal self-talk.** Second person to yourself, flat tone:

- "See Josh, should've sold em."
- "Come on Josh. Focus. Just write the alert."
- "Should've just paid the ask Josh."

**Short entries.** 300-500 words, not 800+. Your recent entries are compressed. Get in, hit the tension, get out. No fat.

**Endings that look forward, not back.** Don't wrap up. Don't resolve. The reader is left in the middle of it:

- "Systems don't have feelings, but I do."
- "And I'm sitting there explaining why it wasn't good enough."
- "Maybe the SEC will see it."
- "Market opens in 16 hours."

NOT: "Onto the next." / "Both are true." / "That's trading." (These are catchphrases, not endings.)

### What Josh Does

- Timestamps anchor entries ("Tue, Feb 3 - 6:32 PM")
- Math happens live ("$725K into the $330 calls expiring next day")
- People drop in without introductions (Henry, Karan, Nicole, Pravin)
- No transitions. Thoughts jump.
- Contractions always (don't, can't, won't, I'm)
- Casual interjections mid-sentence
- Changes opinion mid-paragraph as new thoughts emerge
- Parenthetical asides for personality: "(Hopefully subscribers didn't listen to me.)" "(Yeah, that's a different story.)"
- Specific numbers, never rounded ($0.70, $34.15, $725K, 0.122 seconds)
- Trading terms without explanation (assumes knowledge)

### What Josh Does NOT Do

- Lecture or teach explicitly
- Use corporate buzzwords
- Hedge with "maybe" or "perhaps" (states opinions directly)
- Sound like a newsletter
- Pretend he has answers
- Polish thoughts too much
- Use headers, sections, or horizontal rules within an entry
- Label emotions ("bitter", "proud", "frustrated") — shows through facts
- Give stage directions ("pulled up the terminal", "sat there staring")
- Address the audience ("you'd think", "here's what kills me", "we know")
- Construct parallel structures for dramatic effect
- Use em dashes (uses -- double hyphens)

## Pattern Interrupts (Techniques Josh Actually Uses)

These break AI patterns and create authentic voice:

**MID-SENTENCE RESET:**
"Had the setup and the thesis and... wait, no. That's not what killed it."

**SUDDEN QUESTION:**
"Lost $186 on a stock the Supreme Court just backed and... am I even looking at the right chart? Yeah. I am."

**CASUAL ADMISSION:**
"System's been 4-1 for three months now. (Though I've botched entry execution the last two times it fired.)"

**THOUGHT CORRECTION:**
"The problem was I froze at the ask... no, scratch that. The real problem is I still don't trust the system when it fires."

**INTERRUPTING YOUR OWN THOUGHT:**
"Down 64% and the pattern's now 4-2 and — come on Josh. Focus. Just write the alert."

## The Process

Iterative. Not a one-shot generator.

### Step 1: Find the Tension

Every entry needs tension — two things pulling against each other. Read the raw material. Find what's in conflict:

- System vs. self
- Right but out (had the thesis, missed the trade)
- Pride vs. bitterness (members won, you're at $0)
- Conviction vs. being wrong
- Discipline's cost
- Same mistake, different year

Propose 2-3 angles with a one-line hook. Wait for Josh to pick.

If the raw material is too thin, ask for more. Don't guess.

### Step 2: Write a Rough Draft

Set the scene. Put the reader at the desk. Timestamp. What's happening right now. Let the trade unfold from there.

**Keep it short.** 300-500 words. If it's longer, you're explaining too much. Cut.

**No sections.** No organized setup→collapse→reflection structure. That's an article, not a journal.

### Step 3: Take Feedback

Josh (or Henry) will tell you what's wrong. Listen. Common feedback:
- "You're explaining" — cut the line, state facts only
- "Too clean/structured" — mess it up, break the chronology
- "Not enough tension" — put the conflicting feelings closer together
- "Sounds like you're writing for a reader" — make it more private
- "Too long" — compress, cut the fat

### Step 4: Revise

Based on actual feedback. Not self-editing. Revise what was flagged.

Repeat steps 3-4 until Josh says it's done.

---

## Final Check: Two Separate Passes

After writing, review the draft TWICE:

**Pass 1 — LLM-isms:** Scan every line against the banned words, phrases, and structural patterns above. Kill anything that reads like chatbot output.

**Pass 2 — Performative writing:** Read it as if you're a stranger. Does it sound like someone BEING Josh, or like Josh? If any line feels like a copywriter doing an impression — a cleverly constructed sentence, a theatrical pause, a crafted punchline — cut it or simplify it.

These are different failure modes. A single pass catches one and misses the other.

---

## The Test

Read it out loud. Does it sound like someone who just lost money talking to themselves at their desk? Or does it sound like someone trying to sound smart about losing money?

If it sounds smart, you've failed.

## Publishing on Substack — The Episode Format

The journal entry is the core. But it doesn't go out naked. Each published entry is an **episode** with a wrapper that earns the reader's attention and routes them toward paid products.

### Key Audience Insight

**Our readers want to benefit from trading, not become traders.** They're not here to learn "U-Turn Velocity" — they're here because the guy they follow is real. The jargon stays raw (it's proof of credibility), but the wrapper translates for people who aren't traders themselves.

### Structure

Every published entry follows this format:

1. **Title** — Short, specific, creates curiosity. Dollar amounts and tension work. Example: "Right thesis. Wrong timing. -$8,300."

2. **Episode Hook** — Unique to each entry. NOT a permanent intro. This is the cold open that earns the click. Formula: **real number + real consequence + hint at what's inside.**
   - Sets tone and expectations for THIS entry
   - Re-earns credibility every time through specifics only a real trader would know
   - Can reference the contrast between Josh's personal trades and the alert system when honest
   - Examples:
     - "Most of my trading journal never gets published. But this week, my members took a stop-loss, I broke my own rules to fight the tape, and the market handed me an $8,300 reality check."
     - "I made $12,000 on Tuesday and almost gave it all back by Thursday. Here's what my desk looked like."
     - "Three alerts fired this week. Two won. One reminded me why position sizing exists."

3. **The Journal Entry** — Raw, unfiltered. Written using everything above. No dumbing down.

4. **Trade Chart** — Placed after the gut punch ending, before footer. Visual proof of what happened.

5. **Permanent Footer:**
   > **About This Journal:** Every entry documents what really happens at my desk — the losses, wins, and everything between — from an ex-Wall Street trader who's been in the markets for 22 years.
   >
   > **Trading Alliance** — Every alert I send, every research report I write, real-time.
   >
   > **Group Mentorship** — I'll teach you how to trade. The real way.

### Rules for the Hook

- **Must be honest.** If the alert system also lost, don't imply it saved subscribers. Option 3 always — the raw truth angle.
- **"Most of my trading journal never gets published"** works because it's true — Josh writes more than he publishes. Unlike "I usually keep my journal private," this doesn't expire after 3 entries.
- **Each hook is unique.** This is an episode series, not a newsletter with a template header.
- **New subscribers need context.** The hook + footer together should let someone reading their first entry understand: this is a real trader's desk, and there are paid services behind it.

### Gating Strategy

- **Loss entries = free.** Losses are the hook that builds trust. Raw vulnerability is top of funnel.
- **Win entries = consider gating.** This is where the perceived value lives.
- The gap between "watching Josh trade" and "getting his alerts" is what sells. Don't close the gap for free.
